01 — ORIGIN

What Burry actually did

"I don't look at things the way other people do. I look at the raw data."
— Michael Burry, Scion Capital

In 2005–2007, Burry didn't short mortgage bonds directly. He purchased credit default swaps (CDS) — insurance contracts that paid out if mortgage-backed securities failed. His maximum loss was the premium he paid each quarter, regardless of how wrong he was or how long he waited. His upside was 100x if the thesis played out. He held for two full years while investors screamed at him to close. The thesis was right. The timing was uncertain. The structure absorbed the uncertainty.

The three elements that made it work: (1) Defined maximum loss — only the premium, nothing more. (2) Asymmetric payoff — small outlay, large return if right. (3) Thesis-driven holding — he didn't exit because the price moved against him, only because the thesis changed.

04 — PUT MECHANICS

How the Burry put structure works

01
Identify a structural flaw
Not a price prediction — a thesis about a system that will break. Burry identified that mortgage underwriting standards had collapsed. Our theses: PE firms hold $2.5T in floating-rate debt that becomes unserviceable at 5%+ rates; AI companies at 30–60x revenue face multiple compression from energy costs and rate sensitivity.
02
Wait for two independent signals to confirm
Burry didn't just believe the thesis — he required data. For HYG puts: both HYG credit spreads and the PE equity basket must deteriorate simultaneously. One signal is noise. Two independent streams are signal.
entry = (HYG 5d return < −1.5%) AND (PE basket vs SPY 10d < −5%)
03
Buy OTM puts — pay the premium, define the loss
Strike 5–8% below current price for HYG, 15–25% below for NVDA/PLTR. Out-of-the-money = cheaper premium, larger payoff if right. Expiry 6–9 months — enough time for the thesis to develop. Never buy 30-day puts on a multi-month thesis.
max loss = premium paid · max gain = (strike − price at expiry) × 100 per contract
04
Hold through the pain — exit only on thesis change
Burry held for two years while investors tried to pull their money. The puts may decay for months with no movement. Do not exit because the price moved against you — exit only if the thesis invalidates: Fed cuts rates, peace deal hits, or credit spreads tighten.
05
Tax-efficient execution — Roth IRA only
Put gains are short-term capital gains, taxed as ordinary income. At your attending marginal rate (37%), a 4× win in taxable becomes a 2.5× win after tax. In Roth IRA: entirely tax-free. The structure only works in the right account.
Roth IRA: put gains → tax-free · taxable account: put gains → 37% ordinary income

07 — IRON RULES

The rules that don't bend

  • Total put premium never exceeds 5% of investable assets. If every put expires worthless, you barely notice and the core portfolio is untouched.
  • All puts go in the Roth IRA. Short-term options gains taxed as ordinary income at 37% in taxable. Tax-free in Roth. The structure only works in the right account.
  • Two independent signals must confirm before any put entry. One signal is noise. Two independent streams are the Burry discipline. HYG alone is not enough. PE basket alone is not enough. Both together — enter.
  • Exit on thesis change, not on price movement. If the put is bleeding but the thesis is intact — hold. If the thesis invalidates (Fed cuts, peace deal, credit spreads tighten) — exit regardless of P&L.
  • Buy OTM, not ATM. ATM options are expensive and give limited asymmetry. OTM options are cheap, expire worthless more often, but when the thesis hits — the payoff is 3–10× the premium. That's the Burry structure.
  • Never add to a losing put position. Averaging down on options accelerates premium decay. Size correctly at entry and hold or exit.
  • Cash preservation through June 2026 is non-negotiable. Gap year transition creates cash flow risk. No new put positions until Banner contract is confirmed and first paycheck arrives. The model watches — you wait.

09 — ASSET LOCATION

Where each asset lives — and why the tax code decides

Asset location is the zero-cost optimization most investors ignore. The same asset generates dramatically different after-tax returns depending on which account holds it. At your attending income (~$401K gap year), the wrong location can cost 15–25 percentage points of return annually — on assets you already own.

K-1 trap. PDBC is structured as a limited partnership — it issues a K-1 tax form every year even if you sold nothing. K-1s create phantom income, arrive late (often after April 15), and require complex filing. Inside a 401k: entirely irrelevant. The K-1 disappears.
401K / 457B
Holding PDBC in taxable means annual K-1 headaches, phantom taxable income, and late tax forms every year. Zero benefit. Put PDBC in the 401k — the problem disappears completely.
SCHH / REITs
Ordinary income trap. REITs must distribute 90%+ of income — and those distributions are taxed as ordinary income, not qualified dividends. At your income: 40.8% rate annually, forced whether you want it or not. In 401k: zero annual drag.
